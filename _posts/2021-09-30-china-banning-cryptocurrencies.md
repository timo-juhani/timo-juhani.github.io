China is making efforts to ban cryptocurrency [transactions](https://techcrunch.com/2021/09/24/china-says-all-cryptocurrency-related-transactions-are-illegal/) and [mining](https://news.yahoo.com/china-bans-crypto-trading-mining-155020817.html) based on the risk they present on "economic and financial order".

Cryptos are not [Fiat](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/fiatmoney.asp) currencies. Fiat currencies are government backed and valued by the law of supply and demand and the stability of the government. Whereas Cryptos are backed by the value of the currency itself (scarcity and capability to hold its value for instance).

Cryptos being non-Fiat, it's been shown over and over again that the valuation is highly susceptible to market speculation. For instance, after China's proclamation the value of Bitcoin decreased by third (although recoved by now). Sometimes a tweet has been enough to impact the current valuation significantly. Considering traditional currencies and investment vehicles a drop of third would be considered a major problem. So, no wonder countries that thrive under government's strict steering opt to ban or regulate the crap out of cryptos. 

That said, traditional investment vehicles are not protected of speculation (or manipulation) enabled by distributed systems such as social media campaigns (e.g. Reddit) as we have seen in [AMC](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/AMC) and [Gamestop](https://finance.yahoo.com/quote/GME) cases.

Always remember that economical stability is a major component of security.
