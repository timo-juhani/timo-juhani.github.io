Last week the criminal extortion group Lapsu$ gained quite a bit coverage. In addition to the previous Nvidia hack they have got their hands on Micrsoft’s source code. This is said to cover software like Bing, Cortana and so on. Microsoft’s investigation showed a compromised account that was used for a limited access grant to their environment. Microsoft responded by pointing out that they don’t rely on secrecy of the source code. As this security policy applies to network configuration and routing protocols too we fully support it. Adopt a mindset that your platforms may have been breached already. 
 
Interestingly Microsoft also pointed out that this adversary doesn’t mind covering their tracks. In fact, they are trying to buy credentials online from the employees of the target organization. In addition to Microsoft, Lapsu$ targeted Okta – an IDM as a Service company and Samsung. They already dumped Samsung’s data in the Web. Okta’s case seems similar to other Lapsu$ hacks: a compromised employee account that provided access to a laptop over five day’s period with the impact of roughly 2.5% of Okta’s customers. Protect your credentials with strong access control design. Make sure you monitor admin users’ activities proactively. 
 
Last week British police arrested some of the crew members including its 16 year old leader. The typical OPSEC issues like bragging in the social media and not protecting you indentity permitted the arrests. It’s an interesting side note here that Tor, which is typically used for OPSEC with a false sense of security, has been compromised years ago by the US authorities (backed by a well-known court case from 2017). In short if you own the enter and exit points of the networks you can tell where the target lives and what is the data. In fact, the only thing that Tor does for you - unless you’re governmental user - is to paint a target on your back. We recommend something else if you need to protect your people in the field. 
 
So the lesson here is that OPSEC is a secondary objective if you only want money. 
 
Few things about Ukraine war. Russian gov hackers have been charged by US authorities for hacks against energy sector. The realistic outcome of the charges is to deny access to western and ally countries to, for instance, pursuit career goals in high paying tech jobs. There is a political pattern here: each time US president calls for attention to Russia’s cyber activities a set of new criminal charges against the individual perps are published. It happened in the past and most likely we’ll see it in the future too. 
 
Due to heavy bombing on the cell towers the Russian troops have rolled back to use normal 3G/4G handset for their C2 comms. They are using local SIM cards seemingly giving Ukrainians means to listen in their C2 channels. If they do any lessons learned at all, bombing the cell infra in the beginning for the next operation (wherever it may be) will be avoided to maintain operational confidentiality.
 
The satellite interference has been proved to was a Russian operation. It’s not surprising for us, but it’s something to note for as it expands the pro-longed cyber attacks from the domain of hybrid operations to cyber warfare (“attacks causing comparable harm to traditional warfare”).
