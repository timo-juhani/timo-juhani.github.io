This is the second brief OPSEC post this week. The previous post talked about having secure network connection when working or travelling abroad. This is one is about reducing the magnitude of the tracking in the first place and thereby managing the risk of cyber crime.

Outdoorsy people know that understanding the terrain is the first step to mitigate unknowns and potential risks before they become a problem. The same operating principle applies in the cyber terrain. In short, understanding your attack surface is mandatory in order to see how exposed you are. As a proactive person, you turn things around to your advantage and - instead of having an attack surface - you have a defensive perimeter.

![](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/policy/army/fm/3-21-71/fig5-12.gif)

A defensive perimeter provides 360 degrees of protection. (source: FM 3-21.71)

OSINT or Open-source Intelligence is personal information that is publicly available and can be used to analyze a target individual. OSINT is effective because the amount of personal data that has cumulated over the years is dumbfounding. Social media sites among other networked past times require registration. Some of those expose a lot information publicly by default, which is not according to your best interest. It makes it worse that we often use a very weak tactics on managing our credentials.

Check out [OSINT Framework](https://osintframework.com/), which is an impressive collection of open information sources. For now, let's concetrate on social networks and them encouraging us to share every living vibration. That's fine, but you have to understand what info has cumulated over time and how does it look like as a collection. While individual posts may be harmless, post collection may convey a story you don't want to tell.

> Some business opportunities will be lost due to weak social media posture.

Social media is a quite big portion of the defensive perimeter, you must control it. Having a structure approach makes things easier:

*   Understand your defensive perimeter. Make a list of your social networks you are affiliated with. If you have some technical provess you should use a tool like [Sherlock](https://github.com/sherlock-project/sherlock) to automate this part of the work. Sherlock makes the point clear why re-using same user ID may not be a good idea.
*   Understand your vulnerabilities. Check out you defensive perimeter by using the different views the sites offer like public or friend. For example: too much information about you or your family, collections of posts convey a story you don't want to tell or simply unused profiles.
*   Shut down all accounts you don't need anymore. Within GDPR you have the right to see the information they have on you.
*   Tighten down the privacy settings to a level you are comfortable.
*   Sanitize old and unwanted data, i.e., delete what is not needed anymore.
*   Change the weak passwords. Use different user IDs and hardcore passwords. Password manager like [Keepass](https://keepass.info/) helps there. This helps to evade mass collection of your info.
*   Understand that sites have a tendency of rolling back the default weak privacy settings after a user policy change.

Defensive perimeter should be analysed and hardened periodically. The world, ideas, values, politics, trends, people and you personally change faster than you would like to admit. Therefore, what seemed safe a year ago may raise a red flag today.

Although this post talks from individual's perspective, this is a business issue as well. Some business opportunities will be lost due to weak social media posture. That's all this time. Guard up and stay safe!

P.S. Above does not mean that you can still hide in plain sight or take the POE (proof of existence) tactic that many have adapted succesfully. More on this later.
