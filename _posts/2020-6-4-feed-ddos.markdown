I've been following up the social media ever since the protests started in the US. To set it off: My very limited understanding of the situation and history, doesn't provide me to have a public opinion on it. I'm just making observations on the technology that seems to lay the foundation for messaging and sharing information during an incident like this.

That all said, I noticed a few days back that my feeds started filling from single colored screens that, to my limited understanding, were posted to show support and express solidarity. Psychologist could probably explain how group thinking and safety concepts work in these types of situations. However even as a layman it is safe to assume that expressing opinion, let the reason be any, is important. Luckily we have social media that allows to broadcast one's viewpoint.

The flip side of social media becomes problematic from an engineering perspective - which just might be the worst viewpoint at the moment. Few days back I woke up, checked my feeds and I couldn't help thinking that either my feeds were [DDoS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack)'d or the platform was hacked. For I was browsing for news and information how the situation had evolved over night and all I saw were single colored squares on the feed. Feed DDoS is just like any other DDoS but instead of clogging the network pipes it glogs the information feed very visibly on [application layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OSI_model).

Although expressing support to the people is important, it doesn't come without a cost. The cost in my case was that I couldn't get signifcant updates of the situation anymore. That in turn rendered social media's main capabilties useless. We should be cognizant of that cost.

To manage the cost of relying on social media during incidents we as a countries and individuals should try to find answers to the following questions at minimum:

*   How much trust can be safely put on social media during incidents?
*   How much of the new content provides updated information on the situation?
*   Who are the players that are actively shaping our feeds?
*   Are some of them our adversaries?
*   How do we validate information offered to us?

When there is DDoS in the networks the first response is to get it under control and restore the service. We should try to develop methods to maintain voluntary or involuntary Feed DDoSs so that the important and validated information is still raised to our attention. This incident has made it clear that there is much to do on that front.
