A snappy article was about WhatsApps end to end encryption was published Sep 7. Find it [here](https://www.propublica.org/article/how-facebook-undermines-privacy-protections-for-its-2-billion-whatsapp-users). The expectation is that end user communication is end to end encrypted. But in the light of the article it appears there is a team of people who's sole job is to open flagged messages and read them. 

You could enter a long debate on political science and law issues such as who decides what is improper, who has the authority to investigate and moderate private communications and what are the implications of those two on the design of the society we live in. 

The main thing worth pointing out right now is this: There are authorities in each country who by law should be doing this and no-one else. It's not a good idea to leave the capability to a company who's in the business of information gathering. That's why this article should trigger an alarm in all WhatsApp users.

The recipient of a message can flag it. Once done, it is uploaded along with the last four previous messages to Facebook team for a review. It's unlikely why Facebook couldn't do that at their will with or without using the flag. 

A feature like this leaves the door open for misuses. For example, attacking message groups by adding something "improper" and flagging it for review. It seems that the initial moderation decision is often done by AI because that's the only scalable response. If the moderation request is reviewed by a human they have (it seems) three options: ignore, put the original sender or ban the account. 

WhatsApp doesn't encrypt metadata. But metadata is exactly the data that is most interesting often. Enough said about that.
