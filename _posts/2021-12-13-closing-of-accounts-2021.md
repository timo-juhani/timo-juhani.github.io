It's been months already since I made a last update and the year is in the end. That's why it's time to sit down and reflect a bit so that the next year can be a step forward. This is the closure fort past year.

It was a fifty-fifty year meaning fifty percent time commitment at home and the other fifty at work. I kicked off the year with 7 months paternity leave. Returning to office around August produced a quite hasty fall. Both these periods were unique and had ups and downs.

## Malfunction

During the first quarter of the year when kicking off the long leave, I found myself from a figurative pit. I had worked hard until the day it was time to switch off for 7 months. It was a full stop and I had an extremely hard time to let go off the work and adjust myself to the new pace of living. It turns out that nurturing a wild little caveman is different to what I had on my plate before. The drastic sudden change of the daily program was hard to handle. 

Thinking about it now it is easy to recognize but at the time it wasn't. I felt tired, bleak and had all kinds of physical issues that were clearly a result of prolonged and unmitigated stress. This was January pretty much. On February I realized that something needs to change. On March I realized that I had been sustaining a flame too big and started active stress relief protocol. Something that really woke me up was my dad saying in passing: Have you tried doing nothing today. 

## Patching The OS

I'm not interested being like that. The operating system needs to be patched up and running well:
- Sleep: No less than 7.5 h for me. Make it happen.
- Stretching: Do it almost daily. In the evenings. Keep it below 60 s per stretch. The primary objective is recovery. 
- Sauna: Do it often, but it shouldn't be a battle of will. Start swimming outside around April-May. Use and time intelligently as it can aggravate as much as recover. 
- Meditation: Mind needs sharpening too. More about this later. 
- Walk: Go out alone or with someone and just be there. There doesn't need to be anything else to it.  
- Physical training: It should be something to look for and not a beat down each time. Of course there is a time for the latter, but it can't be each day. You can get pretty strong on a minimal strength routine combined with sprints and some long runs. Limit strength sessions to 30 min if you do it daily. This is feasible on long run and seems to avoid mental and physical fatigue. Anything longer (right now considering the amount of exercise that comes from just juggling the kids) seems to be causing too much inflammation to recover from. 

## Maintenance Mode

Just by looking at the list above tells that I can pursuit things until my own demise, but where I was failing was stopping. Those words from my father keep popping up to my mind and while it made sense I had no idea how to implement it. 

I started implementing daily 10-20 min meditation to my routine least to be affiliated with an increasingly emerging crowd of self-righteous feel good hippies. Rather I remembered that it was something we did after a hard training session in martial arts. 

So I started to fiddle with it and soon realized that it does exactly what my dad proposed. After some minutes of internal chatter - which seems like the mandatory small talk that must happen before you can move on to the beef of the actual agenda - it becomes quiet. It is exactly and nothing but being and doing nothing. I'm a pretty passionate person compared to the regional average. I need tools to slow down and think clearly to have better interactions with people. This last part clearly was the top priority since my little man was next to me unless he was sleeping. 

## Standalone vs. Swarming

The biggest caveat of constant pursuit is to be too result-oriented. Matters become more important than interaction with people. I rediscovered an old lesson. Individual success is not the same thing as team success, i.e., if you need to be in some location on a given time it's not enough if you're there alone and your team is not. Individual success is a good baseline and keeps people motivated, but the most important thing is to succeed as a team. Because in the real life you are almost always working with a team. 

If everyone cares too much about individual success and recognition, you will have a group of individuals that are in competition (in one way or another) with one another. Although good natured competion is necessary, bibical rivalries are counter-productive. Care about team success and you will have a community of people that are happy, self-motivated, understand the importance of their role and feel safe because of it. 

This starts from admiting that group comes first. Any single person and everybody will provide net positive value if they take on roles that are on par with their competence. That's why it's a fact that groups outlast individuals. Someone said that a lone human is a dead human in the nature. Remember this.

## Crypto Mayhem

On April-May period I started to regain interest in technology again. It helped that the both winter and summer turned out to be perfect: a lots of snow and then again warm sunny summer. I enrolled in a crypto currency and blockchain course after being re-energized during the spring. 

I am skeptical about the hype around the topic although I'm convinced that it's here to stay some way or the other. How big of a buble it is? Probably a big one but it's hard to tell how big. Tulips and gold have the case history. Scarcity is not something to invest in. 

One really good sanity check is to ask: What does it produce? For instance, if you think of a saw mill it's easy. It produces planks from timber, which constructurs use to build houses, which people buy so that they are not exposed to the elements and, perhaps, can sell for a profit later on. 

If you ask the same question for NFTs for instance, it turns to promiseware pretty fast: a content producer publishes a valuable article, which trades for a NFT in their website, the value of the NFT is set by bidding, the winner takes the rights to the article home with him. There is no-one to stop from copying the article so the content becomes available for anyone. Why? Because there is no-one (at the moment) who protects the content the same way as the security protects Mona Lisa in Louvre. It doesn't matter what you own if it can be taken from you. This is how countries have disappeared to from maps. It's not enough to say what is yours, you must be able to protect it for the value it produces. 

This said, there might be really great applications for NFTs in Web3 native space. For instance, in games the goods you exchange are virtual so they can be better protected using technical controls too. Basically anything that has value in its digital format in the digital domain is a direct example here, in my opinion.

I wrote a small and silly crypto currency to learn about blockchains. It turned out to be a fun little project to think about something else than I was used to. I was surprised to be contacted about it. I wonder how many people have invested (=speculated) on crypto products without spending time to understand how they work and what are the application opportunities. I'm guessing the fear of missing out has to be pretty high. 

Bitcoin shares the same dilemma as NFTs. US Dollar has the men with guns to protect the interest of the currency wherever you go. Bitcoin doesn't. The only thing that guarantees it is demand and scarcity, which is controlled by the market.

If you do a hard fork on the blockchain you can reset the transaction history as well, much like Ethereum did after the hack. If a community with the blockchain can do this, isn't the whole idea of decentralization lost. If a nation is hard forked the power and the wealth is redistributed. Who gets to do the decision when to do a hard fork? What is it called? A military coup done by the opposing developers?

Try to resist the idea that we're doing something new here. It has the same problems as any system before so it must share similarities that are just masked with techno utopianism ideology.

I'm very bullish on Web3's potential, but I refuse to eat before studying the cuisine better.

## Automation

In June-July I got into studying finance APIs after I got the idea of automating technical analysis of the stocks I'm following. Although, I'm not a trader neither I see more value in understanding the fundamentals of a business, it's still something that you don't want to ignore. 

I found out that this was a rather easy job to accomplish using Yahoo Finance APIs on Python. Combine that with Jupyter Notebooks and you have more than what's needed to crunch the numbers. The remaining task is to punch in the formulas correctly, using the right financial data and making the right interpretations and sanity check of the calculation. Much like we had to do in the university while taking an exam on external accounting.

This summarizes the state of the automation quite well. You still need to know today what the engineers had to know yesterday. Then on top of that you must understand the automation tools and have almost 100 % assurance what you see is what you wanted it to be. This phase is critical because if it's neglected the same mistakes transfer over and over until someone clever catches upon them. Imagine how many bad investment decisions could be made and money lost due to a faulty formula.

The biggest hurdle of automation is that it is a practise at best. There is no theory around it which is why larger organizations with more complexity are facing challenges implementing and using it to solve business problems. 

During the second half of the year we did almost nothing but automation projects: network automation, Infra As Code, orchestration design, automated application stack deployments and so on. Each and everyone of the solution are somewhat different dictated by the use case and desired end result. The key criteria of the project success have been: well-defined use case, tool discussion regarded secondary, close collaboration with the user, almost undefined project scope (within a sprint) and clear idea of the larger direction where we should go. 

The most common problem seen is being stuck on the tool debate. Frankly, it takes nowhere. When they designed the first automatic transmission boxes they wanted to solve the problem of constantly changing gears. The end result was that car manufacturers had their own flavors of doing it which you can still see if you just compare German cars together let alone cars from other regions or electric vehicles. What unifies them is that users of those cars don't need to change gears often, and not how it has been implemented. You need to understand first what you're solving before you can bolt technology on top of it. And to be fair, the latter part is only interesting to the engineers. 

So, what I'm after is: start by defining a clear positive ROI end result and solve it. You'll be exponentially wiser after the first implementation. Later on when there is enough data what works and what doesn't you can start looking into harmonizing tools but that's not the most productive starting point. 

In fact, I had this discussion about programming languages today. Even on coding there is not a single language to cover everything. Instead I got informed that you choose the scope of the coding project and then decide which language works the best. Lucky me, no permanent damage done from neglected C++ studies. 

Sometimes you think you have it right. You may select a set of tools before the build begins only to realize midway through of the sprint that the tools aren't capable to produce what's needed. These scenarios require a fluid mindset and a mission first approach. Just adapt and overcome. Most sprints can be recovered from judgement errors like these.

## Overcast

I've been working trough multiple AWS courses this fall. Although we all have been aware of their offering for years it became very tangible after some time in the lab. I'm fully convinced that it makes less and less sense to build your own production infrastructure if a) you're not a massive organization, b) you have a niché like a government security contract that demands for it. 

This isn't a new phenomenon to be frank. During the 20th century majority of the companies owned their and managed their manufacturing directly. To save the business history section to the experts on that field, I settle for pointing out that outsourcing was recognized as a business strategy around 1989. Computers in 1989 were still simple and especially if you read the books of the early pioneers manufactured in factories (or garages) that were directly owned by the companies. 

Ever since we've been witnessing China emerging as a factory of the world. You name it it's most likely been manufactured at least partially there. Cloud services are products just like any other products and thus respect the same business strategies. IT industry is still catching up with the old tech companies but it's not like there has been an acedemic discovery that would place IT under a different set business management principles. 

Legacy manufacturing has been outsourced to China. Some of it has been moving back to countries of origin during the past ten or so years (approx.) but majority of it still remains in Asia. We'll see the decommission of many small-mid-sized data center operations while the bulk capacity will be outsourced to massive clouds like AWS and Google Cloud. A smaller data center provider can't build something like it without accepting a significant cost. Also that same data center provider if it becomes a customer itself will face something interesting times analyzing the bills and consumption models each cloud has.

Hybrid model makes sense. You still need to retain your classified business data on premise. Even in those strategy the focus will be on outsourced resources while keeping the bare minimum in house. 

## Penguins and Whales

In August Linux operating system turned 30 years. I bet Linus didn't think it would something that touches everyone's lifes today. It's a very Finnish invention. Do something properly, share it with your peers and don't expect anyone patting your back. Just keep working and moving forward. There is a quite clear reason why many of my Finnish felows operate this way. I'm not an expert on that domain so I'll just leave there now. 

The guiding principle of open source (perhaps) spearheaded by Linux is pretty damn hopeful and honest. I build something today which will help you tomorrow so that you don't need to get started from the scratch. This is the way you improve world. Not by being woke in anti-social media and by being upset on every single misfortune or jab that is headed your way each and every single day. Open source approach makes people feel that they are needed and that they belong to a real community of people with similar goals - goals which are productive. 

Looking back this fall, each project has had an open source component to it. Either Linux, Docker (I know what you think) or Git or like in most cases all of them. These three are the foundation for any developing activity whether it's infra as code or just good old app development. It pays to know them well. 

Docker and containers in general is a fascinating technology that fixes most issues we experienced with legacy virtualization. It provides clear improvements on portability, shareability, fault tolerance against system corruption and sudden power cycles, network segments, image sizes, build automation and so on. It's now wonder why all applications that we developed this fall run on Docker. It's a main-stream technology now. 

## Metaverse

In October Facebook changed its name to Meta. Metaverse is a collection of virtual 3D worlds that which provide immersion and interaction. Facebook changing its name to Meta makes you think whether this hype is truly a new Internet in the building. 

It doesn't exist yet though. And it's quite hard to see how the current Web 2.0 players are going to be able to come out of their trenches and do something that is fundametally against their current earnings logic.

Today's web is built on giant corporations' centralized control and ownership of the content created by users of their platforms. People don't own their content or tweets or pages in Google, Twitter or Facebook. Those companies do while feeding their advertisement income using collected user data. 

Web3 on the other hand is currently being designed as a true decentralized network. In this model people own their content and even their metaverses which are exchanged using true digital currencies. This is the dream. It remains to see when and how much it's going to be regulated to conform with the way of current affairs. However, it's childish to think that this Web3 utopia will go in production as the dreamers want. There will be major opposition. 

But even when Web3 goes in production in somewhat diluted form, these incumbents won't be the major players there. Their business models is owning the web whereas this Web3 concept is giving the ownership to the users - as it should be. They won't go away obviously just like we still use GSM even though 5G networks are now online. They just won't be the Web3 native players. Something else will emerge and those are brand new things.

Facebook can be called Meta but it won't have metaverse.
