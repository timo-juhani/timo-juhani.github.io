I seem to read [Agile Manifesto](https://agilemanifesto.org/) periodically. It has to be because different discussions and trainings point you back to review the original manifesto. Although it's starting to be quite dated, I can understand this ask. Agile has become an important product development method which touches most IT projects today and sometimes dominates the discussion. So instead of just reading it through (again), let's review it and see how it holds its ground today.

The Balance Between Value and Devalue
-------------------------------------

Almost everything we do is a balancing act between extremities - and the winner often is the most balanced individual and organization. So when reflecting that viewpoint on the manifesto, I've started to read it as a frustrated but polite bottom to up engineering rebellion. This may take some lines to explain.

The extremity that the manifesto devalues (see picture above) is frustrating to any project team because it's often out of their control and stand in the way of progress. Or at least slows it down.

If you want to build something and are working directly on a customer interface, it's logical that you desire to remove all these speed bumps. You want to concentrate building your product as well and fast you can. This is how you're recognized as a developer. I get that. I know how liberating it is to concentrate on a technical problem without disturbances.

Here's where this gets challenging: Although the manifesto says that it has "come to value" one extremity over another, I argue that not many reads it exactly. I think - rather fear that some reads it so that you can let the other extremity overrun the other one. This on its own is an alarming mindset.

I think the manifesto just tries to make a point that you should put more emphasis on valued extremity and reduce costs on devalued extremity. For instance, let's add more 10% testing hours and reduce the same amount from non-critical documentation or we can afford 8% overdelivery if we avoid contract re-negotiation.

Most propably there is not a winning pattern of how to make a time or cost split between the extremities. It's complicated because organizations, projects and teams are different. Some teams could be happily overcommited to documentation due to legit regulation reasons but some could've just developed a dislike to it since it takes their attention away from engineering.

Leadership and management skills are needed to strike the balance. If there's a vacuum of that and there are no established policies, no wonder people are manifesting in protest to devalue the extremity that is not their duty anyways. However, I argue that in long-term you can't devalue, e.g., proper process and communications and run a business that is handled professionally.

Jokes work because they capture the reality in a fun and often painful manner. So Google around and you find ample of jokes about Agile and implemeting it in a too extreme manner.

I recognize setting the balance on the "valued-vs-devalued axis" as a success criteria. Let's back that up by reviewing the parts of the valued extreme below.

Sprints
-------

Although sprints are a great vehicle for getting stuff done early and often, they don't offer a long-term planning capability. So even if we are emphasizing sprints as a main delivery model, there should be people looking from outside in so that sprint teams are actually accomplishing objectives that not only tackle today's priorities but are aligned to long-term goals.

Looking from the architecture perspective, I don't fully support the idea either that documentation activies are devalued. If you have a long-term goal, you should know where you stand to get there. Without updated documentation understanding the gap is impossible. "That guy knows" is not a proper method of knowledge management. Information should be decentralized since Agile expects decentralized actions.

Therefore, in my opinion, sprints need to produce clear documentation as an outcome. Do we need to spend days and weeks to fine tune massive Word documents? No! But we need to find a way that provides the documentation capability that fills our requirements on the content and reduces time spend on it.

Could we document [as-built](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/as-built#English) or [Infrastructure as Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrastructure_as_code) appended with some great diagrams? Absolutely! As long as it gets done and you are able to reduce costs included to it.

Because sprints require experience, speed and decision-making capability from all individuals so only staff that is trained properly should participate. Sprints are not necessarily the right place for learning on the job.

Finally, you don't always get the most fired up and self-driven people either. Which is fine. We all are different. However, just note that the manifesto and principles that come along are somewhat idealistic about this. In real life, you take what you have and make the most out of it.

This is where this engineering driven manifesto runs under the hem of the management. Sprints require heightened attention and engagement from the managent, in my opinion.

"Plans are useless, but planning is indispensable"
--------------------------------------------------

The idea of working in a collaborative manner rather that concentrating on contract negotiation must come from the fact that [Scrum](https://www.scrum.org/resources/what-is-scrum) has been designed to tackle complex problems. Problems that are not even clearly postulated until you start working on them. If they would be simple they would fall under well-established processes.

That's why plans often fail on the face of complexity. Planning on the other hand is extremely important. I don't think Manifesto's "responding to change over following a plan" is good guidance. Experience shows it should be more like "adjust your planning as the situation unfolds".

You should try to be aggressive rather reactive which means you adjust your planning and make micro decisions as the situation unfolds. Planning helps to identify the need of reaction, gives you reaction options and gives you time to think before the reaction must be decided. This general principle applies to almost everything we do.

Individuals Over Processes
--------------------------

Without processes we would simply be as good as we are as individuals. So if something works really well, it should be documented on high level and deployed in the organization. This way it becomes a standard and a measurable reputation of the organization.

On the other hand, individuals should have decision making capability so that decisions can be made as close to action as possible. The main challenge is that you have to make sure people can differentiate a good decision from a bad decision. This can be helped with training and experience but still at best depends on the individual.

That's why, I believe having a high-level process that allows local decision-making may be producing the best performance at the time of writing. Process should be known and there may be some well-known procedures but you should know what you can change and how much. The process should be further developed (ideally) while the product is developed. There should be same motivation to improve both.

Certainly we shouldn't try to put individual decision making compete against processess because they are what we have in terms of organization performance. They both are factors in the same equation.

Conclusions
-----------

The original manifesto is already old (2001). To set the intent better it should be reviewed and renewed. Clearly, even though the industry is still shifting, lessons learned have been made. It should be pointed out that the [12 principles](https://agilemanifesto.org/principles.html) give more guidance on the objectives, but like the original manifesto, they demand balancing to avoid poor results.

When it comes to writing manifestos in the future, I think good relationships in the chain of command beats them. Writing a paper and calling it manifesto reads like a peaceful mutiny which alone should be a sign that something is not right.

We learn as we go.
