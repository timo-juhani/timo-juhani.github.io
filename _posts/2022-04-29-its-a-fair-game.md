It’s hard to realize (partly because it’s hard to measure) how much different groups are hacking Russia now. If you are following the feed you can see multiple successful attacks per week. The general trend is increasing. It seems to be a community understanding that it’s fair and just to do so and in fact it would be backwards not to. There may be another occurrence like this in the history but not perhaps as public and as big as the case of Russia. So if your cause is not righteous and not in line with the moral rules of the connected global society there will be individuals and groups chasing to get a payback. Land borders do little to help. You can’t command and oppress something you don’t control.
 
2021 was a record year for the number of zero day vulnerabilities. According to Google’s researchers 58 zero days were exploited last year. This is the highest of their measurement period starting from 2014. In 2020 the same number was 25 which is more than half of 2021’s number. Messaging apps like WhatsApp, Signal and Telegram are considered as interesting targets. The same goes with smartphone spyware and Exchange servers. The rise in the number of zero days could be explained by better detection mechanisms and a changing culture to tell about the vulnerabilities prior to successful hacks becoming public.
 
In US lawmakers have realized the strategic role of the cloud service providers. In fact, there is a development ongoing that would place those companies as part of host nations’ critical infrastructure. We don’t know much more at the moment of writing but this is something to keep on the radar.
 
Web scraping is legal according to a court ruling. It’s a methodology which allows extraction of content and code stored on a web site. Although this has been a common practice for many companies and researchers the court ruling removes the grey area of the activity. For admins and users it’s extremely important to ensure protection of sensitive and private data by protecting user accounts properly. Organizations that use social media for external communications and information sharing should consider hardening and monitoring those user accounts.
 
Consulting company CyberEdge has reported that in their ninth annual Cyberthreat Defense Report covering 1200 responses from 17 countries and 19 industries almost 85% of organizations suffered from a cyber-attack. 63% of the ransomware victims paid the ransom only encouraging the attackers. The top threats were 1) malware, 2) credential abuse or takeover, 3) ransomware and 4) phishing. The biggest obstacles that hinder security were: 1) lack of talent, 2) low staff awareness, 3) integration between solutions and 4) lack of management support. On average organizations allocated 13% of their IT budget to security while IT budgets grew by 5%. A question remains how well that IT budget has been directed to fend off the threats and remove those obstacles. It’s clear but not guaranteed that lion’s share should be on solving those issues.
 
Ransomware and Log4j exploitations are the current top threats that affect customers and have been observed in incident responses involving known APT actors. Theres is an increasing activity on leveraging USB drives to deliver remote access trojans. Changes in tradecraft incude using coding language called Rust. Perhaps because Rust isn’t typically within the capabilities of common Antivirus software.
